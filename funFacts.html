Fun Facts

The Dark Web Is Tiny Compared to the Deep Web

While the Dark Web gets a lot of attention, it is only a small fraction of the Deep Web. The Deep Web includes everything that isn’t indexed by search engines, like private databases, subscription services, and online banking portals. The Dark Web, by comparison, accounts for less than 0.01% of the entire internet.

It Was Invented by the U.S. Government

The Dark Web wasn't originally designed for illicit activities. It was developed by the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory in the mid-1990s to protect government communications. The Tor network, the most popular way to access the Dark Web, still receives funding from the U.S. government for its privacy-preserving potential.

It's a Haven for Free Speech

In countries with heavy censorship, the Dark Web provides a safe space for people to express their opinions, organize protests, and access restricted information. This has made it a critical tool for activists and journalists in oppressive regimes.

The Dark Web Has Its Own Search Engines

Even though it's not indexed by traditional search engines like Google, the Dark Web has its own search tools, such as Ahmia and DuckDuckGo’s .onion version. However, these search engines often return incomplete results, and finding specific sites can still be a challenge.

Cryptocurrency Thrives on the Dark Web

The anonymity provided by cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin made them the preferred payment method for transactions on the Dark Web. Today, other privacy-focused cryptocurrencies, such as Monero and Zcash, are also gaining popularity in this space.

Onion Routing Is More Literal Than You Think

The Tor network, which is used to access the Dark Web, gets its name from "The Onion Router." The onion metaphor represents the multiple layers of encryption that protect users' anonymity-just like peeling back an onion.
